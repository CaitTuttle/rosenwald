Introduction

Concept #1:

Today's school rankings can be predicted by a schools demographic population Race, Food Insecurity, and Income were some of the main factors we examined and are a better measure of community affluence than a school's success.

Concept #2:

School rankings have been used to punish (to put it simply) poor, Black schools. School rankings need to be more comprehensive, and it is necessary to address structural inequities Define Structural Inequities to improve school outcomes.

Concept #3:

Rosenwald schoolsQuick synopsis of RW schools are some of the first examples of schools that have considered the health, wellbeing, and connectedness of communities.

Our overarching question:

In what ways can public schools incorporate the holisticA comprehensive approach that considers all aspects of a student's development. strategies of Rosenwald schools to redefine and improve the measurement of school success and better meet student needs? (Link to recommendations)

Takeaways

*Please note that the New/Inclusive Rankings are not intended to be a complete analysis of school success. It is rather created to start conversations about how county rankings that use gross outcomes (i.e. test scores, graduation rates) are not an accurate measure of success, but rather a measure of affluence. A more comprehensive measurement is necessary.

Methodology

We know these measures are outdated and racialized, so to identify some issues with these measures, we created a list of things we believed may be predictors of these outcomes. For example, poverty, food insecurity, race, and homelessness may be predictors of these outcomes. Using multiple linear regression, we tested to what extent these factors (we had a list of factors amounting to more than 30) can predict the outcomes we were looking at (the roadmap of needs rankings). We eliminated factors that had a p value over .05 and any factors that demonstrated multicollinearity, and ultimately found that our testing data (a list of thirty factors) could predict the given outcomes with 67.7%% accuracy and the trained data (a list of 13 factors) with 78% accuracy. This indicates that the measures of county success are actually measures of community affluence, not good measures of school success or effort.

History

What are Rosenwald Schools?

  • 51 years after the Civil War (ended in 1861), when slavery supposedly ended.
  • 16 years after Plessy v Furguson case (1896): created “separate but equal” doctrine, which said segregation was constitutional.
  • Julius Rosenwald: white, Jewish philanthropist from the north, established the Rosenwald Fund.
  • Booker T. Washington-a Black ex-slave who became known for his founding of Tuskegee University, met with Julius Rosenwald and convinced him to invest in schools for African Americans, as education is transformational, and opportunities were limited for African Americans in the rural south.
  • The Rosenwald Fund involved a grant matching program.
  • Black communities raised money for schools, and Rosenwald would “match” these contributions by donating additional money to their fund. Black communities raised money and cultivated resources for the schools in many ways including:
    • Raising and selling goats and hogs
    • “Box parties” where neighbors would bid on boxed lunches
    • Black landowners sometimes cut trees for wood for the schools or offered parts of their land where the school could be placed
  • Community Engagement:
    • Teachers were highly involved with the school's local community, and teachers prioritized relationships with students' families
    • Families contributed labor and money to the construction and maintenance of schools
  • Standardized Design
    • Schools always followed a building plan. Priorities were often natural light, ventilation, and durability.
    • They were typically one-room or few-room buildings
  • Taught academic and non-academic subjects including:
    • Academic subjects (reading, writing, arithmetic)
    • Technical skills and vocational training
    • Gender-specific skills (i.e. cooking, cleaning, making fires)
    • Current events and debates
  • Nearly 5K schools were established in the southern US states.
  • By the end of the program approximately 36% of Black children could have attended school.ining
  • Improved literacy rates
  • When given access to Rosenwald Schools, Black students stayed in school for an average of a year longer.
  • Attendance rates for Black students increased, likely due to higher-quality schools.

From The Rosenwald Fund: Review for the two year period 1944-1946, courtesy of Duke Archives “ The chief activities of the Julius Rosenwald Fund were… (1) education of teachers, both Negro and white, for service in the rural schools of the South; (2) awarding of fellowships to Negroes and to white Southerners of special promise in their various fields of work and interest; (3) operation of a greatly expanded program aimed at the improvement of race relations throughout the county.”

More about Russell School

Russell School was built in 1926 to serve as a school for local residents, but in 1945 it transformed from a school into an active community center.

After 1945, Russell School hosted many events for families and community members. Alum reported the following activities:

  • Marbles for the kids
  • Fish frys
  • The may pole
  • Playing in the creek across the street
  • Going “fishing.” This entails throwing a fishing line through the school window while adults stand on the other side and attach small prizes, such as candy, to the line.

One alum reported that their favorite subject was social studies. At this school, social studies involved reading the newspaper, and since they were donated materials, the newspapers were often a week old

“The only thing I remember was that this is where we went as a member of the church. When I was young, I just remember this is where we came to eat good food. I didn't know anything about it being a school until my mother started doing research.”

  • Negro Rural School Fund of the Anna T. Jeanes Foundation, known as the Jeanes Fund, began in 1907 when Quaker Anna T. Jeanes donated $1 million to improve education for Black students in rural America.
  • The grant sponsored teachers, who did a variety of things over the course of years including:
    • Training other teachers on skills such as wood working, domestic skills, and mat making
    • Serving as academic advisors
    • Ultimately, they served as assistants to school board officials to direct Black schools.
  • These teachers were extremely involved, influential, and admired in local communities and often lived with those they worked with.
  • The board of trustees was racially integrated, became international, and included many prominent figures such as:

Caswell County

  • Caswell County, North Carolina, was established in 1777.
  • The Caswell Training School was a school that operated from 1934 to 1969. According to the book “Their Highest Potential,” this school focuses on the importance of dedicated teachers and the principal who believed their jobs extended well beyond the classroom and to the community’s parents, who worked to support the school. According to Vanessa Walker (author of the book), the relationship between the school and the community was mutually dependent.
    • The parents sacrificed financially to meet the school's needs, and teachers and administrators put in extra time for professional development, specialized student assistance, and home visits.
  • A new project has been implemented in Caswell County, where memories of educational roots are captured in six Rosenwald schools (Beulah, Blackwell, Dortmund, New Ephesus, Milton, and Yancyville). The point of this project is to connect education and the arts to retell and relive Caswell’s African American past and how they debuted in the passport of traditions, doctrines, core values, and cultural indifferences.

Rosenwald Interviews

Mother of a Virginia Native

The interviewee described how much she enjoyed her experience as a student. She understood the expectations that were placed on her by her teachers and parents and, from there, was able to teach the same to her children.

Community Engagement

  • “Well, the community was close-knit and everyone in the community knew each other. We went to each other's houses, we walked with each other to choir rehearsal and then the church.”

Enriched Learning

  • “The only thing that helped me was home economics; I learned how to sew and to cook.” We had English and taught the proper way to talk.”
  • “We had plays in the school; each holiday, we had a play at the end of the school. I participated in all the plays through school.”
