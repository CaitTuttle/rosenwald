Takeaways

*Please note that the Equity Rankings are not intended to be a complete analysis of school success. It is rather created to start conversations about how county rankings that use gross outcomes (i.e. test scores, graduation rates) are not an accurate measure of success, but rather a measure of affluence. A more comprehensive measurement is necessary.

Durham County

"The biggest thing we can do, and this seems to me to be our mission, is to empty our lives and character into our children, thereby making them better and wiser citizens than we are or had an opportunity to be.”-Aaron McDuffie Moore (1863-1923) a prominent community leader in Durham, North Carolina, known for his work that promoted a comprehensive education, economic and social justice, and medicine for Black citizens.

There were a total of 18 Rosenwald schools in Durham, NC. The funding was obtained by Aaron McDuffie Moore.

Russel School

The Russell School, in Durham, North Carolina, sits on a lawn on a bright, sunny day. (Captured June 2024)
A spacious room in the Russell School features original objects, including a chalkboard, window panes, a student desk, and objects purchased or donated by local Black community organizations throughout the years, such as chairs and a piano. (Captured June 2024)
Donated church pews sit in the other room in the Russell School. In 1945, when the room stopped functioning as a school room for upper grades, the room served as a center where the community gathered. (Captured June 2024)
Russell School's two teacher community school floor plan is printed on white paper. (Captured June 2024)
Windows in Russell School were made and placed strategically, with a glazing process that protects individual window panes, and windows were situated from east to west to optimize natural light in the absence of electricity. (Captured June 2024)
An original Russell School desk sits in the lower grades classroom. (Captured June 2024)
The Russell School taught students some gender specific roles. Boys brought in wood and started the fire in a potbelly stove similar to the replica shown above while girls supported teachers with administrative tasks. (Captured June 2024)
Durham County School Board meeting minutes, where Gep Mack appeared before the school board to request that the school board approve the Russell school for an extended school year, are hung on the walls of the Russell School. (Captured June 2024)
The "Buy A Chair Campaign" started in 1946 to preserve the Russell School as a historic location after its closure. Donors contributed $3.50, the cost of a chair, and had their names written on them. This initiative reflects the organized fundraising during the Rosenwald era. (Captured June 2024)

Aaron McDuffie Moore: (1863-1923) a prominent community leader in Durham, North Carolina, known for his work that promoted a comprehensive education, economic and social justice, and medicine for Black citizens.

Read his biography: Aaron McDuffie Moore- Blake Hill-Saya, Rep. G.K. Butterfield, and C. Eileen Watts Welch:
  • Born a free man, Dr. A.M. Moore was a highly educated man who used his experience to promote medical and educational access for Black citizens in Durham.
  • He was Durham's first Black physician, responsible for the opening of Lincoln Hospital, where he served Black patients and where he trained doctors, both Black and white.
  • He is responsible for bringing Rosenwald grants and the Jeanes Fund to Durham county.
  • He was the founder of Durham's Black Wall Street, which was a hub of Black-owned businesses, some notable ones including:
    • NC Mutual Life Insurance Co.
    • Mechanics and Farmers Bank (M&F)
    • The Carolina Theatre

Hayti: the center of commercial, economic, and social activity for Black citizens of Durham, North Carolina

CAPTION
  • Hayti had a self sufficient Black middle class population supported by all kinds of jobs, there were over 200 Black businesses
  • The area was rich with culture and activity, with theaters, hotels, and entertainment. The piedmont blues was born here.
  • This area has changed drastically due to factors such as “Urban Renewal,” which eliminated large portions of the area. The creation of highway 147 in the center of the area contributed to its decline.
  • There is a cultural center to preserve its history. See: Home - Hayti Heritage Center

“Today there is a singular group in Durham where a black man may get up in the morning from a mattress made by black men, in a house which a black man built out of lumber which black men cut and planed; he may put on a suit which he bought at a colored haberdashery and socks knit as a colored mill; he may cook victuals from a colored grocery on a stove which black men fashioned; he may earn his leaving working for colored men, be sick in a colored hospital, and buried from a colored church; and the Negro insurance society will pay his widow enough to keep his children in a colored school. This is surely progress.” -Excerpt from W.E.B. Du Bois’ The Upbuilding of Black Durham. The success of the Negroes and Their Value to a Tolerant and Helpful Southern City

Jeanes Fund: a grant that aimed to improve the condition of education for African Americans by sponsoring influential teachers.

CAPTION
  • Negro Rural School Fund of the Anna T. Jeanes Foundation, known as the Jeanes Fund, began in 1907 when Quaker Anna T. Jeanes donated $1 million to improve education for Black students in rural America.
  • The grant sponsored teachers, who did a variety of things over the course of years including:
    • Training other teachers on skills such as wood working, domestic skills, and mat making
    • Serving as academic advisors
    • Ultimately, they served as assistants to school board officials to direct Black schools.
  • These teachers were extremely involved, influential, and admired in local communities and often lived with those they worked with.
  • The board of trustees was racially integrated, became international, and included many prominent figures such as:
    • Booker T Washington
    • William P. Few
    • George W. Clinton
    • William Howard Taft
    • George Foster Peabody
    • James H. Dillard
    • Walter Hines Page
    • Andrew Carnegie

More interesting history from Martin County

Williamston lies in the heart of Eastern North Carolina's Black Belt on the southern bank of the Roanoke River, one hundred miles east of the state capital of Raleigh and nearly as far south of Norfolk, Virginia.

  • A town in Martin County that faced decades of segregation and racial injustice. Williamston became the focus of the Civil Rights Movement beginning with the Williamston Freedom Movement in 1963 Protesters gathered at the Green Memorial Church led by Sarah Hall and Golden Fink.
    • The protest began in the summer of 1963. For 32 consecutive days, 400 African American people marched uptown to the courthouse singing and praying demanding the abolishment of Jim Crow law(Separate but unequal seating and services in public accommodations, to end discrimination in employment, and a measure of courtesy in their official dealings with whites.)
  • In 1963 blacks and whites each made up half of the town's population of just under 7,000.
  • Martin County's African American men tried to earn a living in the timber industry or by growing, harvesting, and “processing” tobacco and peanuts, the area's two principal cash crops. Black women worked in the fields, and in many cases served as “private household workers- as maids cleaning the houses of their white employers and often playing a primary role in raising their children.
  • Students from Martin County would immediately leave following their graduation. Many students would head to the bus station and move to Williamston. Williamson's population grew by over 1/3 between the 1950s and 1960.
  • Lack of education narrowed job opportunities for many African Americans, despite the protesting, members of the county school board did little to encourage black education
  • Black schoolteachers struggled to remain anonymous under the rigid oversight of the country's white superintendent.
  • Most of Williamston's African Americans were working class, working poor, and, not infrequently, poor seeking work.
  • A reporter visiting Williamston in the summer of 1964 described what he found as “two separate and distinct towns… two conflicting and incompatible realities that have somehow collided over a common geography.”
  • The Fifteenth Amendment has been a fixture of African American struggle in the decades following the wave of disfranchisement triggered by the Wilmington white supremacy campaign at the turn of the century.
  • Williamston black community institutions stressed the importance of the ballot, and, by the 1960s, five years before the passage of the Voting Rights Act, about two in five blacks legally eligible to vote had succeeded in registering in Martin County.

Caswell County

  • Caswell County, North Carolina, was established in 1777.
  • The Caswell Training School was a school that operated from 1934 to 1969. According to the book “Their Highest Potential,” this school focuses on the importance of dedicated teachers and the principal who believed their jobs extended well beyond the classroom and to the community’s parents, who worked to support the school. According to Vanessa Walker (author of the book), the relationship between the school and the community was mutually dependent.
    • The parents sacrificed financially to meet the school's needs, and teachers and administrators put in extra time for professional development, specialized student assistance, and home visits.
  • A new project has been implemented in Caswell County, where memories of educational roots are captured in six Rosenwald schools (Beulah, Blackwell, Dortmund, New Ephesus, Milton, and Yancyville). The point of this project is to connect education and the arts to retell and relive Caswell’s African American past and how they debuted in the passport of traditions, doctrines, core values, and cultural indifferences.

History

What are Rosenwald Schools?

When did Rosenwald happen(Schools existed approximately from 1912-1932)? (Relative to Historical Events)

  • 51 years after the Civil War (ended in 1861), when slavery supposedly ended.
  • 16 years after Plessy v Furguson case (1896): created “separate but equal” doctrine, which said segregation was constitutional.

In 1912, Julius Rosenwald and Booker T. Washington met for the first time, which began the creation of the Rosenwald Schools.

People to Remember

Julius Rosenwald: white, Jewish philanthropist from the north, established the Rosenwald Fund.

Booker T. Washington-a Black ex-slave who became known for his founding of Tuskegee University, met with Julius Rosenwald and convinced him to invest in schools for African Americans, as education is transformational, and opportunities were limited for African Americans in the rural south.

Black Community Fundraising

  • The Rosenwald Fund involved a grant matching program. Black communities raised money for schools, and Rosenwald would “match” these contributions by donating additional money to their fund.
  • Black communities raised money and cultivated resources for the schools in many ways including:
    • Raising and selling goats and hogs
    • “Box parties” where neighbors would bid on boxed lunches
    • Black landowners sometimes cut trees for wood for the schools or offered parts of their land where the school could be placed

Rosenwald School Characteristics

  • Community Engagement:
    • Teachers were highly involved with the school's local community, and teachers prioritized relationships with students' families
    • Families contributed labor and money to the construction and maintenance of schools
  • Standardized Design
    • Schools always followed a building plan. Priorities were often natural light, ventilation, and durability.
    • They were typically one-room or few-room buildings
  • Taught academic and non-academic subjects including:
    • Academic subjects (reading, writing, arithmetic)
    • Technical skills and vocational training
    • Gender-specific skills (i.e. cooking, cleaning, making fires)
    • Current events and debates

Impact

  • Nearly 5K schools were established in the southern US states.
  • By the end of the program approximately 36% of Black children could have attended school.ining
  • Improved literacy rates
  • When given access to Rosenwald Schools, Black students stayed in school for an average of a year longer.
  • Attendance rates for Black students increased, likely due to higher-quality schools.

From The Rosenwald Fund: Review for the two year period 1944-1946, courtesy of Duke Archives “ The chief activities of the Julius Rosenwald Fund were… (1) education of teachers, both Negro and white, for service in the rural schools of the South; (2) awarding of fellowships to Negroes and to white Southerners of special promise in their various fields of work and interest; (3) operation of a greatly expanded program aimed at the improvement of race relations throughout the county.”
